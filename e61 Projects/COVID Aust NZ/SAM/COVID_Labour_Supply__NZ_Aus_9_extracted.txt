Abstract
This paper examines the labour supply effects of the JobSeeker Coronavirus
Supplement introduced by the Australian Government in 2020, a COVID-19
policy measure that almost doubled the primary unemployment benefit. To
isolate the policy’s labour market impact, we use a natural comparison group:
New Zealand citizens in Australia who faced identical conditions but were ineli-
gible for the unemployment benefit.Using a difference-in-differences approach,
we find that the Supplement reduced job-finding rates among recipients by
19% and increased job separations by 64%. We find no subsequent increase
in labour earnings among recipients. To interpret these results, we develop a
search-and-matching model with heterogeneity in benefit eligibility, estimat-
ing that most of the labour supply response stemmed from pandemic-related
shocks that increased work disutility rather than benefit generosity. Our find-
ings suggest that even in a severe downturn, more generous unemployment
benefits dampen job search incentives. However, this trade-off may be mit-
igated (and even yield net social benefits) when public health risks justify
reduced labour force participation.

1 Introduction
The COVID-19 pandemic triggered unprecedented economic disruption globally,
prompting governments to implement exceptional fiscal responses to support house-
holds and businesses. A key element of these responses was the expansion of unem-
ployment insurance benefits, which raised important questions about the trade-offs
between providing income support and maintaining labour market incentives. While
enhanced benefits cushioned income shocks during a period of severe health risk and
economic uncertainty, they may have also influenced labour supply decisions in ways
that merit careful empirical investigation.
This paper examines the labour market effects of Australia’s temporary expan-
sion of unemployment benefits during the COVID-19 pandemic. Specifically, we
study the introduction of the JobSeeker Coronavirus Supplement, a near-doubling
of unemployment benefits that increased the standard payment by AU$550 per fort-
night (approximately US$375) beginning in April 2020. Using a novel identification
strategy that leverages a natural experiment arising from differential eligibility rules
for Australian and New Zealand citizens residing in Australia, we identify the causal
effects of this benefit enhancement on labour market dynamics during the pandemic.
Our findings provide compelling evidence that even during a severe economic
downturn with significant health risks, unemployment benefit generosity substan-
tially influences labour market behaviour. Using matched administrative data, we
find that the introduction of the Coronavirus Supplement reduced job-finding rates
by 19% and increased job separations by 64% among benefit-eligible workers relative
to similar ineligible workers. These effects correspond to an extensive margin labour
supply elasticity of -0.21, suggesting that a 10% increase in unemployment benefits
leads to approximately a 2.1% reduction in job-finding rates. Importantly, we find
no evidence that the additional time spent unemployed translated into better job
matches, as measured by future earnings outcomes for benefit recipients.
Our research contributes to a substantial literature on the labour market effects
of unemployment insurance generosity. The theoretical foundations of job search

has generally confirmed these predictions, with most studies finding elasticities of un-

job-finding rates as workers search less intensively or become more selective ( Mari-

of unemployment rises relative to employment ( Hayward et al. , 2025).
However, the applicability of these findings to severe economic downturns – par-

tion. Several studies of the COVID-19 period in the United States found limited
disincentive effects from expanded benefits ( Marinescu et al. , 2021), though others
documented non-trivial responses ( Ganong et al. , 2024). In the Australian context,
this appeared to be confirmed by public data at the time, as the rate of job finding
during 2020 was similar to previous years, suggesting increased benefits did not influ-
ence labour supply behaviour ( Borland, 2020). The unique nature of the pandemic,
with its combination of severe health risks and mandated economic shutdowns, may
have fundamentally altered the relationship between benefit generosity and labour
supply decisions in ways that warrant careful investigation.
The Australian context offers distinct advantages for studying these questions.
First, unlike many other developed economies, Australia’s unemployment benefit sys-
tem provides flat-rate payments rather than wage-related benefits, creating substan-
tial variation in replacement rates across recipients. Second, Australia implemented
one of the most generous benefit expansions globally during the pandemic, with the
Coronavirus Supplement nearly doubling the base unemployment payment. Third,
and most importantly for our identification strategy, Australia’s unique social secu-
rity arrangements for New Zealand citizens living in Australia creates a compelling
natural experiment: New Zealanders on special category visas were ineligible for the
JobSeeker Payment (and hence the Coronavirus Supplement) despite facing identi-

Our empirical approach combines propensity score matching with difference-in-
differences estimation to compare labour market outcomes between Australian citi-
zens receiving the JobSeeker Payment and similar New Zealand citizens residing in
Australia. This methodology allows us to isolate the causal effect of benefit generos-
ity while controlling for contemporaneous labour market shocks that affected both
groups similarly. To further disentangle the mechanisms driving our results, we
exploit policy variation in the reimposition of mutual obligation requirements (job
search conditions) across Australian states to separate the effects of benefit levels
from conditionality requirements.
This paper makes several contributions to the literature. First, we provide clean
causal estimates of the labour supply effects of unemployment benefit generosity
during a major economic and health crisis. Our findings suggest that even during
periods of severely constrained labour demand and heightened health risks, ben-
efit generosity significantly influences labour supply decisions. Second, our unique
identification strategy, which leverages differential eligibility rules between otherwise
similar populations experiencing identical local labour market conditions, addresses
key identification challenges that have plagued previous studies. Third, we contribute
to the sparse literature on the effects of unemployment benefits on job separations,
an important but understudied margin of labour supply response. Finally, we pro-
vide evidence on whether longer unemployment durations during the pandemic led
to improved job matches, informing the debate on the potential productivity benefits
of more generous unemployment insurance.
While our difference-in-differences approach provides strong causal evidence, it
relies on the parallel trends assumption – that in the absence of the treatment, out-
comes for Australian and New Zealand citizens would have evolved similarly over
time. Although our matching procedure addresses observable differences, individ-
uals in different eligibility groups may still be affected differentially by unobserved
shocks, particularly during a complex crisis like the pandemic. For example, ineli-

Our model indicates that the increase in the disutility of work was the primary

Our findings add nuance to the ongoing policy debate about optimal unemploy-
ment insurance design during economic crises. While the labour supply effects we
document might be considered undesirable in normal economic conditions, they may
have been welfare-enhancing during the pandemic by reducing workplace contacts
and virus transmission. However, our results also suggest that policymakers face
meaningful trade-offs between income support and labour market attachment even
during severe downturns, highlighting the importance of carefully calibrating benefit
levels and duration to specific economic conditions.

2 Institutional Setting
This section outlines the institutional setting relevant to our analysis. We begin
with a brief overview of Australia’s benefit system in Section 2.1. Section 2.2 then
provides a detailed account of changes to the primary unemployment benefit during
the COVID-19 pandemic, alongside other key COVID policy responses discussed in
Section 2.3. Finally, Section 2.4 explains why the design of Australia’s benefit system
makes New Zealanders a strong control group for our analysis.
2.1 Australia’s Benefit System
For individuals who experience temporary job loss, Australia has a three-tier income
support system: a primary payment (i.e. the JobSeeker Payment), a second tier
of means tested family payments (i.e. Family Tax Benefit A), and a third tier of
supplements (i.e. Energy Supplement, Commonwealth Rent Assistance). 1
As shown in Clarke and Nolan (2023), in Australia approximately a third of
unemployed individuals receive the JobSeeker payment and nearly half receive some
form of income support. As these payments are means and asset tested, exclusions
are usually based on the individual being deemed to have suﬀicient assets or a partner
with suﬀicient earnings to support the household.
During COVID both the payment rate and these exclusion criteria were adjusted
due to the nature of the crisis. We describe this in greater detail below.
2.2 Changes to Income Support during 2020
In response to the economic challenges presented by the COVID-19 pandemic, the
Australian government implemented several temporary income support measures

2.2.1 JobSeeker Payment
The JobSeeker Payment (known as NewStart up until the 20th of March 2020) is
Australia’s main unemployment benefit. It provides financial support to individuals
aged 22 to age pension age who are actively seeking employment or unable to work
due to illness or disability. To access the payment, you must meet certain eligibility
criteria, including an Income Test, Asset Tests, partnership status, parenthood status
and Mutual Obligations requirements.
Prior to the pandemic, the base fortnightly rate for a single adult without depen-
dents was AUD$565.70. However, in anticipation of increased unemployment due to
COVID-19, the JobSeeker Payment was expanded by including a temporary Coro-
navirus Supplement, significantly increasing recipients’ total income. At the same
time, Mutual Obligations requirements and the Assets Tests were lifted, the income
test became less stringent, and some administrative changes were made such that
more people could access the payment more easily while easing the administrative
burden on Services Australia 2.

2.3 Other relevant government support
2.3.1 JobKeeper program
The JobKeeper Payment was a wage subsidy designed to support businesses directly
impacted by COVID-19 restrictions. It’s main goals were to: Support business and
job survival while health restrictions were in place; Preserve employer-employee re-
lationships to protect valuable organisational and intangible capital, and; Provide
income support to businesses and workers. Between March 30, 2020, and September
27, 2020, eligible employers received AUD 1,500 per fortnight per retained employee.
After this period, payments became dependent on the number of hours worked by
employees. From September 28, 2020, full-time employees received AUD 1,200 per
fortnight, while part-time employees (20 hours or fewer per week) received AUD
750. In January 2021, these rates were reduced again until the program concluded
in March 2021.
2.3.2 Economic Support Payment
The Economic Support Payment provided additional lump-sum payments to Aus-
tralians already receiving certain government benefits, including the Age Pension,
JobSeeker Payment, and Disability Support Pension. These payments were designed
to offer additional financial assistance to more vulnerable households. Eligible indi-
viduals received two rounds of AUD 750 payments in April and July 2020, followed
by a third AUD 250 payment in December 2020 and a fourth in early 2021. Impor-
tantly, from the second round of the payment, income support recipients who were
receiving the Coronavirus Supplement were not eligible for the ESP.
2.3.3 Early Release of Superannuation
In addition to direct payments, the Australian government implemented the Early
Release of Superannuation scheme, which allowed individuals facing financial hard-
ship due to COVID-19 to access their retirement savings early. The scheme permitted
eligible individuals to withdraw up to $10,000 from their superannuation accounts

While this program provided immediate liquidity for those in need, it had potential
long-term implications for retirement savings, as early withdrawals could diminish
the compounding growth of individuals’ superannuation balances.
2.4 New Zealanders in Australia
The identification in this micronote depends on the use of New Zealanders as a
control group.
The relationship between Australia and New Zealand is unique. In the 1900
Commonwealth of Australia Act, New Zealand was included as one of the States
(Commonwealth of Australia , 1900), and due to this in the Australian Constitution
New Zealanders are also given special protections.
In Australia, New Zealanders are given a special visa on arrival (Special Cate-
gory Visa - subclass 444) and are free to work permanently in Australia. The visa
is cancelled and renewed as New Zealanders leave and return from Australia. New
Zealanders also have access to Medicare and the Family Tax Benefit. However, New
Zealanders are not eligible for transfer payments (either the JobSeeker payment
or payments for services like the National Disability Insurance Scheme). Time lim-
ited eligibility is given after 10 years, with a New Zealander able to receive up to
six months of JobSeeker payments. Prior to 2001, New Zealanders were effectively
treated as Australian citizens with full access to these payments.
During COVID some support systems could be received by New Zealanders even
though other non-citizens were ineligible - namely full Medicare access, Early Release
of Superannuation, the JobKeeper program, and one-off payments associated with
the Family Tax Benefit.
In normal economic times a New Zealander who leaves or loses their job would be
able to travel back to New Zealand and receive the New Zealand JobSeeker payment
after a stand-down period. However, during COVID it was extremely diﬀicult to
move to New Zealand - with both the New Zealand and Australian borders shut,

bookings.

3.2 Sample Construction
From this integrated dataset, we identify two primary groups:
1. Treatment group: Australian citizens who received the JobSeeker Payment
at any point during 2020
2. Control group : New Zealand citizens on Special Category visas (subclass
444) who had been in Australia for less than 10 years (to ensure ineligibility
for JSP) and remained in Australia throughout 2020
To focus our analysis on individuals with comparable labour market attachment,
we restrict our sample to those who experienced at least four consecutive weeks out
of work during 2020. We further exclude individuals who transitioned to businesses
that only appeared in the STP data after June 2020 to avoid compositional changes
in our sample due to the progressive adoption of STP reporting.
Our initial dataset includes 3,252,076 Australians and 80,005 New Zealanders
who experienced time out of work during 2020. After implementing our matching
procedure (described in Section 4), our final analytical sample consists of 22,873
Australian JSP recipients matched with 22,859 comparable New Zealand citizens
(See Table 2).

4 Empirical Methodology
We exploit the unique policy environment created during the COVID-19 pandemic
to identify the causal effect of unemployment benefit increases on labour market out-
comes. Our research design leverages a compelling institutional feature of Australia’s
social security system: New Zealand citizens living in Australia (on Special Cate-
gory Visas) were ineligible for the JobSeeker Payment despite facing identical labour
market conditions and pandemic-related health risks as Australian citizens. When
the Australian government introduced the Coronavirus Supplement – a temporary
AU$550 per fortnight increase to the JobSeeker Payment announced on March 22,
2020 – this policy created a natural experiment with a well-defined treatment group
(Australian JSP recipients) and control group (comparable New Zealand citizens in
Australia).
This identification strategy allows us to address a key challenge in the unemploy-
ment insurance literature: disentangling the causal impact of benefit changes from
concurrent macroeconomic shocks. During economic downturns, governments typi-
cally increase benefit generosity precisely when labour market conditions deteriorate,
making it diﬀicult to isolate the labour supply effects of benefits from demand-side

factors. Our approach circumvents this issue because both treatment and control
groups experienced the same macroeconomic conditions but differed only in their
eligibility for the benefits.
Our empirical approach proceeds in two main steps. First, we use propensity score
matching to create comparable groups of Australian JSP recipients and New Zealand
citizens based on observable characteristics including geographic location, age, in-
dustry, occupation, partnership status, and income. Second, we apply difference-
in-differences and regression discontinuity designs to estimate the causal effect of
the Coronavirus Supplement on job-finding and separation rates. This approach al-
lows us to isolate the labour supply effects of benefit changes from contemporaneous
demand-side shocks, as both groups faced similar labour market conditions but only
Australians were eligible for the JSP and COVID Supplement.
4.1 Matching Procedure
Despite our natural experiment setting, New Zealanders and Australians may dif-
fer on observable characteristics that could influence labour market outcomes. To
address this potential confounding, we implement a nearest-neighbour propensity
score matching procedure to create comparable treatment and control groups. We
match individuals based on characteristics relevant to both JSP eligibility and labour
market outcomes, including:
• Geographic location (Statistical Area Level 4 and State)
• Age (in 10-year bins)
• Industry (1-digit ANZSIC code)
• Occupation (1- and 2-digit ANZSCO codes)
• Partnership status
• Income ventile
• Partner’s income ventile (where applicable)

representativeness, it ensures our comparison groups are similar on observable char-

We define the treatment date as 22 March 2020, when the Coronavirus Supple-

choice reflects the forward-looking nature of job search decisions, whereby individu-
als adjust their behaviour based on expected future benefit levels as soon as policy
changes are announced.
The validity of our DiD estimates relies on the key assumption: parallel trends
which assumption requires that, in the absence of the Coronavirus Supplement,
labour market outcomes for Australians and New Zealanders would have evolved
similarly over time. Visual inspection of the pre-treatment trends in job-finding and
separation rates (see Figures 1 and 2) suggests broadly parallel movements prior
to the policy announcement, providing initial support for this assumption. Further-
more, both groups were subject to identical macroeconomic conditions, public health

To validate our identification strategy and assess the robustness of our findings, we
conduct several additional analyses that strengthen our causal interpretation and

6.1 Regression Discontinuity Approaches
To complement our DiD analysis, we implement a regression discontinuity design
(RDD) that focuses on the sharp change in policy at the announcement date. A
32

===== PAGE 33 =====
WorkinprogressDo notcite
standard RDD approach models the outcome as:
Ygt = α + τ Dt + β1(Xt) + β2(Xt × Dt) + εgt (7)
where Dt is an indicator for the post-policy period, Xt represents time relative to the
policy announcement, and we allow for differential linear trends before and after the
announcement by including the interaction term (Xt × Dt). However, a key iden-
tification challenge in this context is that multiple events occurred simultaneously
at our threshold date – the announcement of the Coronavirus Supplement coincided
with rising COVID-19 uncertainty, the introduction of lockdowns, and other pol-
icy interventions. This confounding would undermine a standard RDD approach.
To address this challenge, we exploit the fact that most of these concurrent shocks
– business employment intentions, pandemic fears, lockdown restrictions – affected
both New Zealanders and Australians in Australia equally, while only Australians
gained access to the enhanced benefits. We therefore extend our analysis to a regres-
sion discontinuity difference-in-differences (RD-DiD) framework, which estimates the
difference in discontinuities between Australians and New Zealanders at the policy
announcement date:
Ygt =α + β1Dg + β2Dt + β3(Xt) + β4(Xt × Dt)+ (8)
β5(Dg × Dt) + β6(Dg × Xt) + β7(Dg × Xt × Dt) + εgt (9)
This specification offers several advantages over our baseline DiD approach. First, it
allows for differential time trends between the two groups, accounting for potentially
divergent trajectories. Second, it permits these trends to differ before and after the
policy change, which is particularly important given the temporary nature of the
Coronavirus Supplement. Since the present value of remaining on benefits would
decline as the expected end date approached, we would anticipate the treatment
effect to attenuate over time – a dynamic that our RD-DiD specification can capture
through the three-way interaction term β7. In this model, β5 captures the immediate
discontinuous effect at the announcement date, providing an alternative estimate of

JobKeeper wage subsidy program was introduced shortly after the Supplement an-
nouncement (on March 30, 2020) and may have influenced observed separation rates.
While both Australians and New Zealanders had access to JobKeeper, its interac-
tion with the Coronavirus Supplement for Australians may have produced compound
effects on labour market decisions.

including a dummy variable ( JK month) for the first month of the JobKeeper program,

also include an interaction between this variable and Australian citizenship to capture
differential responses to JobKeeper between the two groups. The augmented model
is:

(β5 = 0. 0314) reveals that Australians experienced even higher separation rates dur-

covered by both policies.

7 Structural model
In our empirical analysis we compare two groups of individuals, one who is eligible
for the JSP and one that is ineligible due to citizenship.
We are interested in understanding the response of the first group to a temporary
increase in the JSP rate, and use ineligible individuals as a control group. This
assumes that these two groups would have reacted in a similar way in the absence
of the payment rate increase.
However, absent the payment change, the fact that one group would receive
income support while another doesn’t may generate a bias in two countervailing
ways:
• Due to the lack of social insurance, the ineligible group will always search
more intensely to re-enter employment. As this shock reduces labour demand,
this difference in search intensity will translate into a smaller difference in job-
finding rates – leading to a downward bias in the estimate of the disincentive
effect.
• Alternatively, if the eligible group has greater capacity to avoid hazardous work
(due to income support), and the pandemic increased the disutility of work,
their lower job-finding rate might upwardly bias the estimate of the disincentive
effect.
To understand the nature of these biases, the mechanisms behind the observed
changes in behaviour during COVID, and the generalisability of our estimated labour
supply response, we construct a search and matching model. Specifically, we use
a stylised Diamond–Mortensen–Pissarides (DMP) search-and-matching framework
augmented with endogenous search effort and concave disutility of search effort .
During the COVID-19 pandemic, a range of factors increased the perceived costs
of labour market participation. Empirical evidence points to heightened health con-
cerns (Brinca et al. , 2021), caregiving constraints due to school closures, and changes
in work-life preferences ( McDermott and Hansen , 2021). These effects are consistent
42

===== PAGE 43 =====
WorkinprogressDo notcite
with macroeconomic models incorporating preference shifters, where the utility from
leisure rises (or equivalently, the disutility of work increases) in response to pandemic
risks.
In our model, this is captured via two parameters: the search disutility param-
eter and non-employment utility. An increase in search disutility reflects reduced
willingness or ability to engage in active job search—due to psychological costs,
logistical barriers, or elevated health risks. Meanwhile, a rise in non-employment
utility captures the increased relative value of staying out of work, whether from
safety considerations or shifts in household preferences. Together, these parameters
allow us to quantify how much of the observed decline in job-finding rates stems from
pandemic-related preference changes rather than benefit generosity alone.
Furthermore, we allow the change in non-employment utility to vary by type, to
investigate the role of differential preferences in driving our observed results.
7.1 Model Setup
Time is discrete and infinite, indexed in weeks t = 0 , 1, 2, . . . . There is a unit
measure of workers, comprised of two types: a fraction π are eligible to receive
an unemployment benefit following job loss (type e) and the remaining 1 − π are
ineligible (type i). All workers are risk-neutral and identical in productivity. Firms
do not and cannot discriminate between these two types of workers. Firms and
workers meet through a frictional labour market with search and matching frictions
as described below.
Each employed worker produces a fixed output y per period. Wages are exoge-
nous (no Nash bargaining), reflecting short-run wage rigidity. For simplicity we
assume all workers earn the same wage w, set to a constant fraction of output based
on the labour income share in Australia (e.g. w = 0 . 55 y). Unemployed workers re-
ceive income that depends on type: type e individuals are eligible for a benefit, while
type i receive no benefit. Specifically, let bj(t) denote the per-period unemployment

Unemployed individuals also derive utility from non-work (leisure or home pro-

understanding differential labour supply responses.
All agents discount future payoffs at factor β ∈ (0, 1) per week. Jobs end ex-
ogenously at a separation probability δ each period (e.g. layoffs or exogenous quits
occur with probability δ). There is no on-the-job search or recall of jobs in this
model; separated workers become unemployed and must search for a new job.

7.5 Solution Method and Simulation of the COVID Shock
We now describe how we implement the temporary COVID shock in the model and
solve for the transition path. The pandemic shock is introduced as an unanticipated,
temporary change in certain model parameters, lasting for T periods (weeks) before a
return to normal conditions. In particular, during the shock we incorporate two key
changes observed in 2020: (i) an increase in the benefit level for eligible workers ( rt
rises, nearly doubling be), and (ii) an increase in the disutility of work (equivalently, a
rise in the utility of non-employment lj) for the recipient groups, capturing pandemic-

We also calibrate the model on the average pre-COVID and COVID job-finding

transmission from work for both agents.
The shock is assumed to begin unexpectedly (agents do not anticipate it while
making decisions before time 0), but once it starts, agents know its total duration
T and anticipate the timing of recovery. At the end of the shock ( t = T ), the
replacement rate rt and leisure values lj(t) revert to their baseline (pre-pandemic)

the shock is anticipated in this backward step, the value functions incorpo-

the supplement approaches, agents know benefits will drop and may increase
search effort in advance).

8 Conclusion
This study provides new evidence on how a large, temporary increase in unem-
ployment benefits influenced labour market behaviour during the COVID-19 pan-
demic, leveraging New Zealanders residing in Australia as a control group to isolate
the effects of increased unemployment benefits on job-finding and separation rates.
Our findings indicate a significant 19% decline in job-finding rates and a 64% in-
crease in separation rates among Australians, emphasising the disincentive effects of
higher benefit levels. These results are consistent with international evidence and
underscore the policy trade-offs between providing financial support and maintaining
labour market incentives.
Structural analysis confirms these results – and suggests that higher benefit pay-
ments would tend to have a larger labour supply effect in normal economic circum-
stances.
However, importantly both approaches show that most of this decline in job-
finding during COVID was not due to the policy alone. By accounting for pandemic-
specific factors, such as elevated health risks and limited job opportunities. The
search-and-matching model suggests that the pure policy effect was smaller (between
1.7 and 3ppts) than the 5ppt drop in job-finding rates, with the majority of the impact
stemming from broader pandemic conditions. This highlights the value of structural
models in distinguishing policy effects from confounding shocks – though such models
necessarily rest on assumptions and cannot fully disentangle all behavioural drivers
without richer data.
These results are consistent with the broader analysis of the behavioural im-
plications of COVID supports in Australia. ( Sainsbury et al. , 2022) estimate the
labour supply responses to the early superannuation withdrawal scheme, finding
larger labour supply effects. Both estimates suggest that, even during a period of
restricted labour demand like the COVID pandemic, temporary changes to benefit
support systems can influence labour market behaviour.

From a policy perspective, the findings reveal a clear trade-off. While the Sup-
plement provided essential income support during a public health emergency, it also
reduced labour market attachment without delivering clear gains in job match qual-
ity or future earnings. However, the fact that there were labour market responses
during a pandemic does not suggest the policies were poorly designed or poorly tar-
geted. In fact, higher levels of income support that enable individuals to opt out
of work may be desirable when faced with a pandemic - given that COVID is a
communicable disease and a reduction in human contact mitigates it’s transmission.
Crucially, the disincentive effects of generous benefits appear to have been muted,
and perhaps socially beneficial in the pandemic context, where reduced workplace
contact may have supported public health goals. These results imply that optimal
unemployment insurance design may vary with context: in normal times, such large
drops in job-finding would be undesirable; in a pandemic, they may be welfare-
enhancing.
Overall, our findings demonstrate that even in periods of weak labour demand,
unemployment benefits influence behaviour. But the context in which policies are
introduced fundamentally shapes both their costs and benefits. Well-targeted, con-
ditional, and time-sensitive benefit expansions may help strike the right balance
between protection and incentives in future crises.
